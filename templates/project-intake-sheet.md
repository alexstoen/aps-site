# Common Intake Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Not confirming exact deliverables before the flight**
"Photos and video" is not a deliverable specification. Confirm quantity, format, resolution, edit level, and intended use before you fly. A client who says "photos" may expect 40 edited selects; another may expect 8. Never assume.

**2. Not capturing success criteria**
The success criteria question is the most skipped field on any intake form and the most valuable. When a client can articulate exactly what success looks like, every decision on shoot day — altitude, angle, timing, framing — becomes easier and faster. When they cannot, ask until they can.

**3. Assuming the client understands drone limitations**
Clients frequently do not know that drones cannot hover safely over crowds, cannot fly in winds above 25 mph, cannot operate during rain, or cannot legally fly in certain airspaces without authorization. Educating the client during intake prevents day-of conflict and scope disputes.

**4. Skipping airspace verification until the day of the flight**
Discovering a TFR, active LAANC denial, or class D shelf at 6:00am on shoot day is avoidable. Verify airspace at booking, recheck the week before, and recheck the morning of.

**5. Missing branding or formatting requirements**
Commercial clients, real estate agents, and event organizers frequently have branding standards that affect how deliverables must be formatted — specific aspect ratios, color profiles, no competitor signage in frame, watermark placement, vertical vs. horizontal outputs. Ask at intake, not at delivery.

**6. Not documenting special requests in writing**
Verbal special requests ("just get the mountain in the background") become disputed deliverables when the client later says it was the whole point of the shoot. Write every request down and read them back before ending the intake call.

**7. Not discussing turnaround expectations explicitly**
Clients arrive with assumptions about delivery speed. A real estate agent with a listing going live in 24 hours and a construction client expecting photos "whenever" have opposite timelines. State your turnaround, confirm the hard deadline, and flag any jobs that require rush scheduling.

**8. Not confirming site access before driving to the location**
Gated communities, active job sites, and rural properties all have access constraints. Arriving without a gate code, access pass, or on-site contact is avoidable. Confirm access in writing before departure.

**9. Not asking whether a listing is going live before or after delivery**
For real estate, the listing date drives everything — photo count, editing style, turnaround urgency, and whether twilight shots are worth scheduling separately. Ask the listing date as part of standard intake.

**10. Marking a project complete before all deliverables are confirmed delivered and received**
Sent does not mean received. Confirm the client opened the delivery link, was able to download files, and has no questions before closing out the project and archiving the files.
